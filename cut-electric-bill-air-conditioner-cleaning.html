A TIKTOK user has explained how some simple maintenance work can save homeowners hundreds on the cost of air conditioning.

Gary Frost, who goes by garyfrostmusic on TikTok, explained that cleaning the coils on an outdoor AC condenser can reap big rewards.

He often posts DIY project advice and AC maintenance tips in addition to music clips.

As Gary laid out in a recent video, cleaning your AC coils is a simple task, and has major benefits for your wallet and central air system.

AC units clogged with dirt and dust work less efficiently and consume more energy to churn out cool air.

The dust essentially works as insulation, trapping heat inside the unit that should be escaping.

Before opening the AC unit and cleaning the inside, Gary turned the power off so he could work safely.

Gary said he sprayed Nu-Brite coil cleaner on his unit before rinsing it with water, which costs about $18 per bottle.

Similar products sell for under $10 at Home Depot and will work effectively.

While chemical solutions and water can effectively clean your AC, avoid using products that weren't explicitly made for air conditioners.

According to Hustle, harsh chemicals like ammonia and bleach can damage your AC.

He also attached a $50 foam gun nozzle to his hose for a deeper clean, but a typical hose head will work fine.

By rinsing the interior and exterior of the coils, Gary was able to clear out most of the built-up dust and debris preventing his AC from operating efficiently.

He said the deep clean can save him $200 per month on his electric bill, although savings will vary based on your current bill, the state of your condenser, and the price of electricity in your state.

EnergySaver, the US Department of Energy's consumer advocacy wing, recommends cleaning your air conditioner at least once per year.

In addition, EnergySaver recommends cleaning your air conditioner's filters at least once every two months during seasons that it's in use.

Other ways to reduce your energy bill

Energy bills are rising around the country, with the average household spending more than $2,000 on utilities each year.

The average nominal retail electricity price increased 4.3% in 2021, the fastest single-year increase since 2008, according to the U.S. Energy Information Administration.

There are simple ways to make your home more energy-efficient on your own.

The Department of Energy has a list of DIY projects you can use to save energy and estimated how much each will cost to finish.

For instance, weatherstripping your windows will cost $5 to $10 and an hour of your time but can cut your energy costs by 5% to 10%.

Energy Saver estimates you can save 10% on cooling costs by turning the temperature up by 7° to 10° for eight hours a day, such as when you're away.

Other simple tricks like closing the shades can reduce heat by nearly 33% according to the Department of Energy.

And households can save more than $100 annually by unplugging appliances that consume electricity even when turned off.
