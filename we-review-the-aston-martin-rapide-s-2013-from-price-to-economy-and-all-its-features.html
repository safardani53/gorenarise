We review the Aston Martin Rapide S (2013) from price to economy and all it

 Posted on June 30, 2024  |  6 minutes  |  1268 words  |  Martina Birk

EVER wondered what 007 would drive if he settled down and became a family man?

Wonder no more. Aston Martin this week launched the sensational Rapide S, the world’s most beautiful four-seater sports car.

It’s also the perfect present for Aston Martin to celebrate their 100th anniversary this year.

Motors this week has a first drive of the Rapide S on UK roads – and an in-depth interview with the German boss who has transformed the famous British firm in the past decade.

THE BIG INTERVIEW

HE is the man who has done more for Aston Martin than James Bond.

For the past 13 years, Ulrich Bez has been the mastermind who turned Aston Martin from sporting pretender to the coolest car brand on the planet.

But the German engineer who learned his trade with Porsche and BMW admits: “I didn’t know anything about Aston when I arrived, I’d never even driven an Aston Martin car.”

He says he was attracted by the brand.

He says: “Aston Martin cars didn’t mean anything to me, but it was a brand I knew, like most people, because of James Bond.

“But I knew if we got the right products it could become a global brand that could challenge Ferrari.”

Bez also knew he was not everyone’s choice to be the boss and smiles when he remembers a senior executive openly questioning a German being in charge of the British brand.

But Bez has a reputation for not only being a brilliant engineer but also a perfectionist used to getting his own way and delivering great cars.

His impressive CV includes playing a key role in the development of the original BMW 3.5 and 7 Series, followed by the legendary 993 at Porsche.

But Aston was the first time Bez had total control of a car company, albeit reporting to their American owners of the time, Ford.

He found a company with a great name but just one sub-standard product, the DB7, and two factories — one a converted barn at Bloxham, Oxon, and the other a rundown facility at Newport Pagnell, Bucks.

Today Aston have a state-of-the-art factory at Gaydon, Warks, a line-up of glamorous models and instead of selling 600 cars a year they sell nearly 4,000.

Bez’s vision has also stretched to Aston’s first £1million-plus supercar, the 1-77, Aston’s first small car in the Cygnet, and their first four-door coupe, the Rapide.

The James Bond franchise and 007 himself has fallen back in love with Aston — Daniel Craig picked up his last month.

But it’s been a turbulent ride on the way. In his first week, Bez caused a strike when he banned workers at the Bloxham factory from eating beside the cars on the factory floor.

The strike was over in a week as the workers became the first to realise that Bez’s word was now law.

It was the first of many contentious decisions by Bez, all geared to his relentless drive for perfection and obsession with the quality he knew was needed.

Bez’s assessment after his first drive of the DB7 in 2000 was simple: “It was not up to my standards. I knew we had to immediately improve everything about the way we built our cars and their quality.”

His next decision was to postpone the launch of the Vanquish for a year because it wasn’t right.

Then he axed a planned mid-engined Aston and replaced it with the DB9 and the V8 Vantage.

But his biggest decision was to start a programme to develop a new body system that would allow Aston to make endless different models off the same chassis.

Bez also knew he had to give Aston a new, modern design identity to get them noticed again.

Brit firm back on top ... thanks to German Ulrich Bez, here with Ken Gibson

He says: “In 2000, no one apart from enthusiasts knew about Aston Martin outside the UK. We had to get a global visibility and I believed that every model had to instantly look like an Aston.”

He reveals that he would have liked a ground-breaking SUV model on the road by now. But he says the global recession of 2008 hit Aston and their Middle Eastern owners hard.

He says: “The economic situation slowed down the steps we wanted to take and put us back years.

“Of course I would like an Aston SUV, it is very important.”

Bez has a clear vision of the next five years, with a target of 7,000 sales a year based on the core sports models such as the DB9, Vanquish, Vantage and newly face-lifted Rapide S, plus hopefully an SUV.

Ironically, the most German of Germans, Ulrich Bez believes his biggest influence at Aston has been one thing.

He says: “Aston Martin now stands for the best of British and people love the firm because it’s British, and that is my biggest pleasure.”

At the age of 70, Bez is clearly not the long-term leader for Aston and he says he will be happy to hand over the reins.

He says: “If there is somebody with the personality, character and vision to be the front man for Aston, that is fine.”

They will have a mighty tough act to follow.

ROAD TEST: ASTON MARTIN RAPIDE S

I LIKE cars that live up to their name and the Rapide S does – big time.

The S stands for Sport, a commodity this car has by the bucket-load.

It also happens to be the most beautiful four-seater sports car in the world – but it now has a much more assertive face thanks to a gaping and menacing grille.

Beautiful ... Aston Martin Rapide S

This sets the sporting tone for the Rapide S that is rounded off by an aggressive rear end, featuring a boot lid flip that counteracts lift at high speeds.

And high speed is what the new Rapide S has been built for – with power from the 6litre V12 engine boosted from 470bhp to 550bhp, cutting the 0 to 62mph time to 5.2 seconds and blasting the top speed up to 190mph.

More Motors

The engine sounds sublime – a deep, glorious burble that explodes into a raging roar when you flatten the accelerator.

To complete the impressive figures, despite the extra power the Rapide S is more economical at 19.9mpg and CO2 emissions have been cut to 332g/km.

Engineers have also worked hard to improve the stability of the car at high speeds and a test drive in the Cotswolds underlined it was very much mission accomplished.

The Rapide S has a balance and agility that defy its 5,020mm length and it handles twisty, demanding country roads with the agility of a pure two-seat sports car.

Luxury Four people can do long journeys at well in excess of 100mph in silence, although the intimate back seats remain best for children or small adults because the S is not really a saloon car.

But anyone sitting inside the Rapide S will feel special as they are bathed in luxury with sports seats finished in a unique black and red interweaved perforated leather alongside carbon fibre and piano black trim.

In many ways, the new Rapide S is the perfect example of Ulrich Bez’s Aston Martin – a compelling mix of beauty, luxury and power with just a dash of four-seat practicality. All you need is £144,950 to buy one.

But the best news is that production of the Rapide S has been moved back to Gaydon, Warks, from Austria, making it very good news for British jobs.
