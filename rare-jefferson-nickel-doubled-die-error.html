Rare Jefferson nickel with double error sells for $300 - how to find one in your spare change too

 Posted on August 3, 2024  |  3 minutes  |  512 words  |  Jenniffer Sheldon

A FIVE-CENT coin from the 1940s was recently sold online for $300.

Collectors were willing to spend so much for this 1943 nickel because it contains a doubled die error.

Coin dies - the metal pieces used to imprint emblems on coins - typically strike coins multiple times to ingrain the image or text.

Doubled die errors occur when the die for one side of the coin is misaligned after the first strike and leaves out-of-place markings.

A die used by the Philadelphia Mint in 1943 to stamp the obverse, or heads, side of nickels was slightly off-kilter.

As a result, the Professional Coin Grading Service (PCGS) estimated that about 35,000 doubled die nickels like this one were mistakenly produced.

Value of the 1943-P doubled die nickel

While other well-known doubled die nickels feature doubling on the reverse, or tails side of the coin, the 1943-P is the only Jefferson nickel to feature obverse doubling.

The 1943-P is also called the "doubled eye" - if you look closely you can see that Jefferson's eye is engraved on the coin twice.

The words and numbers on the obverse side also display some signs of doubling, but this coin is most notorious for its eye error.

Although the coin was uncertified, the seller said it was in "about uncirculated" condition - the best designation a circulated coin can achieve.

"About uncirculated" 1943-P doubled die nickels are worth around $100 to $200, according to PCGS.

This recently sold coin was initially listed for $1 but sold for $300 after receiving 20 bids.

In "uncirculated" condition, 1943 doubled dies have sold for upwards of $1,000 at auction.

What other Jefferson nickels are valuable?

If you don't have that particular coin, there are other Jefferson pieces that can be worth in the hundreds as well.

Like the 1943 doubled die, almost all of the most valuable five-cent pieces are error coins.

These include the Henning Jefferson nickel, one of which recently sold for $425 at auction.

Henning nickels feature a crack on the lower part of the letter “R” in the word “Pluribus” on their reverse side.

While these are counterfeit, coin collectors value them today due to their iconic history.

Another Jefferson error piece with an embedded copper error on the reverse sold for more than $300.

In addition, The Sun covered a 1984 Jefferson coin mistakenly embedded with copper that sold for $150.

To see if your spare change is worth anything, you can check eBay by searching the full name of the coin, selecting the "sold" listing, and then toggling the search to "highest value".

This way, you'll get a sense of what collectors are willing to pay for your coins.

You can also check price estimates on appraisal sites like USA Coin Book or PCGS.
